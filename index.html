What even is a perpetual future?

Let’s unpack that sentence. When you buy a stock like $NVDA through a regular brokerage, that’s called spot trading: you own actual NVIDIA shares, and you only make money if the price goes up.

A perpetual future (traders call them perps) is different. You never own the asset.

You’re placing a bet on its direction: bet long if you think the price goes up, bet short if you think it drops. Price moves your way, you profit. Price moves against you, you lose.

Spot trading

You buy the horse. You own actual $NVDA shares, and you profit only if the price goes up.

Perpetual futures

You bet on the race. You own nothing, and can make profit on both price going up or down.

Why is it called “perpetual”?

Regular futures contracts have been around since farmers locked in grain prices in the 1800s, and they all share one feature: an expiry date.

A regular future is like a concert ticket, only good for one specific date. When that date arrives, the bet settles and everyone goes home.

A perp is like a subscription: it runs until YOU cancel. No expiry, no settlement date. You can hold a position for nine seconds or nine months.

That’s the “perpetual” part, and it’s the reason perps became the most traded instrument in all of crypto. And now you can trade perps on anything: stocks, commodities, FX, you name it.

Okay, but why would anyone trade these?

With perps, you can potentially make far more money with far less capital. The tool that unlocks this is leverage.

The easiest way to understand it is the race car analogy. Trading without leverage is like driving a normal car. Trading with leverage is like driving a Formula 1 car:

  • You reach your destination much faster.
  • Small movements have a much larger impact.
  • Tiny mistakes become expensive mistakes.

The car isn’t inherently bad. You just need to know how to handle the speed.

What to remember about leverage

  • It’s a dial you choose on every trade, 2x to 100x on most exchanges. Your $100 at 10x = a $1,000 position.
  • The exchange fronts the position, your deposit backs it. That deposit is called margin, and the exchange designed the game so it never loses.
  • All the risk lives in your deposit. Losses drain it first, and if it’s nearly empty the exchange auto-closes your trade. That moment is called liquidation (next section).
  • It amplifies BOTH directions. Leverage doesn’t make you right more often, it raises the stakes on whatever you already are.

The part nobody tells beginners

Here’s the best feature of perps, and weirdly the least talked about: you can never lose more than you put in.

In old-school margin trading, a bad enough night means your broker calls and you OWE money.

With perps, the worst case has a hard floor: the $100 you posted, and not a dollar more. No debt, no phone call.

That’s why small accounts can play this game: $100 captures the upside of $1,000, while $100 stays the absolute most you can lose.

(Real talk though: you can absolutely lose ALL of that $100, and at high leverage you can lose it in minutes. The floor is a floor, not a safety net. Deciding how much to put on the floor at once is basically the whole skill of trading.)

The rules of the game

Most guides skip this part. It’s the most important section in this entire guide, so read it closely.

And there’s one analogy that makes everything click: perps is poker. This one is extremely powerful.

The poker map

  • Leverage = playing bigger tables
  • Margin = your chip stack
  • Funding = paying for a popular seat
  • Liquidation = busting out

You already met leverage: instead of a $1/$2 table, you’re sitting at a $100/$200 table. Same game. Much bigger consequences.

Now the rest of the rules.

Rule 1: Margin is your chip stack

Every trade starts with chips. The money you post to open a position is called margin: that’s your stack for the hand.

Winning hands add chips. Losing hands remove them, in real time.

Rule 2: Funding is paying for a popular seat

If everyone wants a seat at the same table, the casino charges extra for it. The crowded trade pays.

In perps, it works like this: every 8 hours, the crowded side of the bet pays a small fee to the other side. This little fee is the clever trick that keeps a contract with no expiry date glued to the real price.

Everyone wants the same seat? That seat costs extra.

And here’s the secret almost nobody teaches beginners: funding is also a crowd meter. When funding is heavily positive, the long side is packed and literally paying for the privilege.

Historically, the moment the crowd is most confident is exactly when the market is most fragile. You now read a signal most traders ignore.

Rule 3: Liquidation is busting out

Once your chips are gone, you’re out of the tournament. The hand might have eventually gone your way. Doesn’t matter. You’re already eliminated.

Here’s why it happens. Remember how leverage means you control $1,000 with $100? The exchange fronted that position, and the exchange does not lose money.

So the moment your losses get close to draining your chip stack, the exchange force-closes your position and keeps what’s left. No warning, no rebuy.

One night in October 2025, cascading liquidations wiped out over $19 billion in positions in a few hours. Most of those traders weren’t wrong about crypto. They were sitting at bigger tables than their chip stacks could survive.

How much room you have depends entirely on your leverage:

How far the price can move against you before you’re liquidated

2x~50% drop (a catastrophe)
5x~20% drop (a very bad week)
10x~10% drop (a bad day)
20x~5% drop (a bad hour)
50x~2% drop (a bad tweet)

Longer bar = more room to breathe. Read this twice, it’s the most important picture in the guide.

Liquidation isn’t the exchange stealing from you. It’s arithmetic you agreed to the moment you picked your leverage.

Rules 4 and 5: Two things you just need to know exist

The mark price: exchanges use a smoothed, market-wide price to calculate liquidations, so one glitchy second on one exchange can’t wipe you out. Nothing to do here, just good to know you’re protected.

Auto-deleveraging (ADL): in a true market meltdown, exchanges can force-close even WINNING positions. It’s like the casino shutting down the tournament mid-hand and settling everyone up: rare, feels deeply unfair, happens anyway. Worth knowing the rule exists before you sit down.

Uncomfortable truth from inside the industry: at banks and hedge funds, humans mostly don’t click the buttons anymore. Systems follow rules.

Why? Because the biggest account-killer isn’t bad analysis. It’s emotion. Revenge trading after a loss. Panic closing a good position. FOMO entering at 2am.

A rule written down at noon doesn’t panic at midnight.

5 rules I’d give my best friend

  • Risk 1-2% of your account per trade. At 2% you can be wrong 30 times in a row and still be standing.
  • Start at 2-3x leverage. The 50x button will still be there in six months. Scroll back to the liquidation chart if tempted.
  • Know your liquidation price BEFORE you enter. If seeing that number on the chart scares you, your position is too big.
  • Set your own stop loss. A stop is you choosing your exit. Liquidation is the exchange choosing it for you, at a worse price, with none of your input.
  • Your first ten trades are tuition, not income. The goal of trade one is to still be around for trade fifty.
