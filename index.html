What even is a perpetual future?

Let’s unpack that sentence. When you buy a stock like $NVDA through a regular brokerage, that’s called spot trading: you own actual NVIDIA shares, and you only make money if the price goes up.

A perpetual future (traders call them perps) is different. You never own the asset. You’re placing a bet on its direction: bet long if you think the price goes up, bet short if you think it drops. Price moves your way, you profit. Price moves against you, you lose.

Spot trading

You buy the horse. You own actual $NVDA shares, and you profit only if the price goes up.

Perpetual futures

You bet on the race. You own nothing, hold as long as you want, and can profit in BOTH directions.

Why is it called “perpetual”?

Regular futures contracts have been around since farmers locked in grain prices in the 1800s, and they all share one feature: an expiry date. A regular future is like a concert ticket, only good for one specific date. When that date arrives, the bet settles and everyone goes home.

A perp is like a subscription: it runs until YOU cancel. No expiry, no settlement date. You can hold a position for nine seconds or nine months. That’s the “perpetual” part, and it’s the reason perps became the most traded instrument in all of crypto.

Okay, but why would anyone trade these?

One word: leverage.

Leverage is like cranking up the sensitivity in a video game. Same controller, same thumb, but now the smallest flick sends the camera flying. With leverage, every move the market makes hits your account harder.

Every move the market makes hits 10x harder. In BOTH directions.

You put up $100, pick 10x, and you now control a $1,000 position. A 5% move up makes you 50% on your money. You don’t need $50,000 to take a real position. You need $50 and an opinion.

But sensitivity works both ways. A 5% move against you also hits 10x harder. Leverage doesn’t make you right more often, it just raises the stakes on whatever you already are.

The part nobody tells beginners

Here’s the best feature of perps, and weirdly the least talked about: you can never lose more than you put in.

In old-school margin trading, a bad enough night means your broker calls and you OWE money. With perps, the worst case has a hard floor: the $100 you posted, and not a dollar more. No debt, no phone call. That’s why small accounts can play this game: $100 captures the upside of $1,000, while $100 stays the absolute most you can lose.

(Real talk though: you can absolutely lose ALL of that $100, and at high leverage you can lose it in minutes. The floor is a floor, not a safety net. Deciding how much to put on the floor at once is basically the whole skill of trading.)

The rules of the game

This is the part most guides skip, and it’s exactly the part that separates traders who last from traders who donate.

Rule 1: Margin is your health bar

The money you post to open a position is called margin. Think of it as your HP in a game: your wins add to it, your losses drain it, in real time.

Rule 2: Funding is surge pricing

Every 8 hours, the crowded side of the bet pays a small fee to the other side. It works exactly like Uber surge pricing: when too many people want the same ride, that ride gets more expensive. This little fee is the clever trick that keeps a contract with no expiry date glued to the real price.

Too many people piling onto one side? That side pays surge pricing.

And here’s the secret almost nobody teaches beginners: funding is also a crowd meter. When funding is heavily positive, the long side is packed and literally paying for the privilege. Historically, the moment the crowd is most confident is exactly when the market is most fragile. You now read a signal most traders ignore.

Rule 3: Liquidation is your health bar hitting zero

Remember how leverage means you control $1,000 with $100? The exchange fronted you the other $900, and the exchange does not lose money. So the moment your losses get close to draining your margin, the exchange force-closes your position and keeps what’s left. HP hits zero, you’re auto-ejected from the match. No warning, no continues, no respawn.

One night in October 2025, cascading liquidations wiped out over $19 billion in positions in a few hours. Most of those traders weren’t wrong about crypto. Their health bars were just too small for the leverage they picked. How much room you have depends entirely on it:

Liquidation isn’t the exchange stealing from you. It’s arithmetic you agreed to the moment you picked your leverage.

Rules 4 and 5: Two things you just need to know exist

The mark price: exchanges use a smoothed, market-wide price to calculate liquidations, so one glitchy second on one exchange can’t wipe you out. Nothing to do here, just good to know you’re protected.

Auto-deleveraging (ADL): in a true market meltdown, exchanges can force-close even WINNING positions. It’s like an airline bumping passengers off an overbooked flight: rare, feels deeply unfair, happens anyway. Worth knowing the rule exists before you fly.

How do you actually get good at this?

Uncomfortable truth from inside the industry: at banks and hedge funds, humans mostly don’t click the buttons anymore. Systems follow rules, because the biggest account-killer isn’t bad analysis. It’s emotion. Revenge trading after a loss. Panic closing a good position. FOMO entering at 2am.

A rule written down at noon doesn’t panic at midnight.

That’s all algorithmic trading really is. Not “build a robot in Python,” just: decide your rules while you’re calm, then let a system carry them out so 2am-you can’t overrule noon-you. Automation is cruise control for discipline. You still pick the destination, it just keeps your foot from twitching.

5 rules I’d give my best friend

  • Risk 1-2% of your account per trade. At 2% you can be wrong 30 times in a row and still be standing.
  • Start at 2-3x leverage. The 50x button will still be there in six months. Scroll back to the liquidation chart if tempted.
  • Know your liquidation price BEFORE you enter. If seeing that number on the chart scares you, your position is too big.
  • Set your own stop loss. A stop is you choosing your exit. Liquidation is the exchange choosing it for you, at a worse price, with none of your input.
  • Your first ten trades are tuition, not income. The goal of trade one is to still be around for trade fifty.
